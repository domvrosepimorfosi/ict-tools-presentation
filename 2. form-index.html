Comprehensive Guide to Using Google Forms

Google Forms is a 100% free tool from Google that lets you create surveys, quizzes, and registration sheets. It's part of the same family as Google Docs and Google Sheets.

For teachers, its most powerful use is creating self-grading quizzes. You create the questions, set the answers, and the form does the marking for you, saving you hours of work.

Key Advantages for EFL

  • Self-Grading: Saves enormous amounts of time on marking multiple-choice, short-answer, and fill-in-the-blank questions.
  • Instant Feedback: Students can see their score and which questions they got wrong *immediately* after they submit.
  • Excellent Data: You get a summary of the whole class (e.g., "70% of students missed question #5") and can review each student's individual answers.
  • Versatile: It's not just for quizzes. You can use it for class surveys, needs analysis, or as a way for students to submit written work.
  • Accessible: It works on any device (phone, tablet, computer) and is part of their free Google account.
  1. Get a Google Account: Like Docs, Forms is free with a personal Google account (like Gmail).
  2. Go to Google Forms:
    • Type forms.google.com into your browser.
    • Go to drive.google.com (your Google Drive) and click "New" > "Google Forms".
    • Open Gmail and click the "waffle" icon (9 dots) in the top-right corner, then select "Forms".
  3. Start Creating: Click the large "Blank" form button (a rainbow +).
  4. Name Your Form: At the top-left, click on "Untitled form" and give it a name (e.g., "Unit 3 Grammar Quiz").

You build your quiz by adding "questions" (which Google calls "items"). Click the (+) button on the floating side-menu to add a new question.

When you add a question, you choose its type from a dropdown menu. For EFL, the most useful types are:

  • Multiple Choice: The classic. You write the question and several options. You can click one as the correct answer.
  • Checkboxes: Similar to multiple choice, but students can select more than one correct answer. (e.g., "Which of these words are verbs?").
  • Short Answer: Students type a 1- or 2-word answer. This is perfect for fill-in-the-blank. (e.g., "He is ___ to the store. (go)"). You can set "went" as the correct answer.
  • Paragraph: Students type a long-form answer. This is not self-grading, but it's a great way to have students submit a short piece of writing (e.g., "Write 50 words about your weekend.").
  • Dropdown: Functionally the same as "Multiple Choice," but it saves space. Good for long lists (e.g., "Choose the correct preposition").

Adding Other Elements (from the floating menu)

  • Add Image (Picture Icon): You can add an image *as* the question. This is fantastic for vocabulary quizzes (e.g., Show a picture of an 'apple' and ask "What is this?").
  • Add Video (Play Button Icon): You can embed a YouTube video directly into the form. You can then add questions *about* the video, creating a perfect listening comprehension test.

This is the most important step! By default, a form is just a survey. You must turn it into a quiz.

  1. At the top of your form, click the "Settings" tab.
  2. Find the option "Make this a quiz" and turn it ON.
  3. Review the Quiz Settings:
    • Release marks: Choose "Immediately after each submission" (so students get instant feedback) or "Later, after manual review" (if you have paragraph questions).
    • Respondent settings: It's a good idea to UNTICK "Missed questions" and "Correct answers" if you don't want students to share the answers with others. You can leave "Point values" ticked.
  4. Go back to the "Questions" tab.

Setting the Answer Key

Now that it's a quiz, when you click on a question, you will see a new link at the bottom called "Answer key".

  1. Click "Answer key" for that question.
  2. Select the correct answer (or answers) by clicking on it.
  3. Assign a point value (e.g., "1" point).
  4. (Optional but great!) Click "Add answer feedback". Here, you can type feedback for *incorrect* answers (e.g., "Remember, we use 'an' before a vowel!") or *correct* answers ("Great job!").
  5. Click "Done" and move to the next question.
Important Tip: At the top of every question, there is a toggle switch for "Required." It's a good idea to turn this ON for every question so that students cannot accidentally skip one.

1. Sharing with Students

  1. When your quiz is finished, click the big purple "Send" button (top right).
  2. Ignore the "Email" tab. Click the Link icon (üîó) in the middle.
  3. This will show you a long link. Click the box "Shorten URL" to get a much nicer, shorter link.
  4. Click "Copy."
  5. You can now paste this link into an email, Google Classroom, Teams, or write it on the board for students to type in.

2. Viewing the Results

As students submit their answers, the results appear in real-time on your form.

  1. Open your quiz (in editing mode).
  2. At the top, click the "Responses" tab.
  3. You will see three sub-tabs:
    • Summary: This is amazing. It shows you charts and graphs for the whole class. You can instantly see the average score, the most frequently missed questions, etc.
    • Question: This lets you review one question at a time and see all the answers given for it.
    • Individual: This lets you look at one student's completed quiz. This is where you would go to manually grade their "Paragraph" answers.
  4. Export to Sheets: Click the green Google Sheets icon (a cross). This will create a perfect spreadsheet of all your students, their answers, and their scores, which you can save as your official mark book.
