Comprehensive Guide to Creating a Padlet

Padlet is an online "virtual wall" or "digital bulletin board". It's an incredibly simple and flexible tool that allows you (and your students) to post "notes" on a shared digital space. These notes aren't just text‚Äîthey can be images, videos, links, documents, drawings, and more.

Think of it as a shared webpage that anyone can add a sticky note to, all in real-time.

The Free "Neon" Plan

The free plan is excellent for getting started, but it has one major limitation:

  • 3 Padlets Limit: You can only have 3 active Padlets at one time.
  • This is NOT a "3 per month" limit. It's 3 total. To make a 4th Padlet, you must delete an old one.
  • Workaround: You can "archive" an old Padlet to make a new one, or you can export your old Padlet as a PDF/Image before deleting it.
  • Storage: You also get 20MB of storage per upload, which is more than enough for images and documents but not for large video files (though you can link to YouTube videos for free).
  1. Sign Up: Go to padlet.com and sign up for a free "Neon" plan. You can use your Google, Microsoft, or any email account.
  2. Make a Padlet: From your dashboard, click the "Make a Padlet" button.
  3. Choose Your Format (Very Important): Padlet's main power is its different layouts.
    • Wall: The classic. Posts appear like bricks in a wall. Good for brainstorming.
    • Shelf (Highly Recommended): This is the most organized format. You create columns with titles (e.g., "Grammar," "Vocabulary," "Questions"). Students then post their notes *under* the correct column.
    • Timeline: Posts are organized along a horizontal line. Perfect for historical events or story sequencing.
    • Canvas: A free-form canvas where you can drag posts around and connect them with arrows. Great for mind maps.
    • Map: Students drop pins on a real-world map. (e.g., "Where I went on holiday" or "Where this food comes from").
    • Stream: A simple, top-to-bottom blog-style feed.

After you choose a format, a "Modify" panel will open. This is where you set up your wall.

Key Settings to Change

  • Title: Give your Padlet a clear title (e.g., "B1 Class Brainstorm - The Environment").
  • Description: Add clear instructions (e.g., "Post one idea for how we can reduce waste.").
  • Icon: Add an emoji to your title to make it visual and fun.
  • Wallpaper: Change the background to a color, gradient, or image to match your topic.

Posting Settings (Important for Class Management)

  • Attribution: If you turn this ON, it will show the name of the student (if they are logged in) above their post. If it's OFF, all posts are anonymous. (Anonymous is great for shy students, while attribution is better for assessment).
  • Comments: Turn ON to allow students to write comments underneath each other's posts. (Great for peer feedback).
  • Reactions: You can allow students to "Like," "Vote," or "Grade" (give stars) to posts. "Like" is a simple and effective engagement tool.
  • Require Approval (Moderation): If you turn this ON, no post will appear on the wall until you (the teacher) click "Approve." This is essential if you are worried about inappropriate content.
  • Filter Profanity: Automatically replaces bad words with emojis. It's a good idea to turn this ON.

1. Sharing with Students

Once your Padlet is set up, you need to invite students to it.

  1. Click the "Share" button (top right).
  2. A panel opens. Click "Change privacy".
    • Secret (Default & Recommended): Anyone who has the link can access the Padlet. It's not searchable on Google. This is the easiest option.
    • Password: Same as "Secret," but they also need a password you set.
    • Private: Only you and people you *specifically* add by email can access it.
  3. Next, set "Visitor permissions" to "Can write". This allows students to add posts. (If you only want them to read, set it to "Can read").
  4. Get the Link: Go back to the main Share panel and click "Copy link to clipboard".
  5. Paste this link into your Google Classroom, email, or chat.

2. How Students Post

When students open the link, they don't need to sign in. They simply double-click anywhere on the wall (or click the (+) icon). A new post (a "sticky note") will appear, and they can:

  • Write a title and text.
  • Upload a file (like a PDF or Word doc).
  • Take a photo with their device's camera (selfie, picture of their notebook).
  • Link to a website.
  • Search for an image or GIF directly from Padlet.
  • ...and much more, like add a drawing, record their voice, or add a map.
  1. Brainstorming (Wall Format):

    Warm-up: "What words do you know related to 'food'?" Students add all their ideas to the wall. You can then drag similar words together (verbs, fruit, etc.).

  2. KWL Chart (Shelf Format):

    Create three columns: "What we Know," "What we Want to know," "What we Learned." Students post notes in the first two columns before a lesson, and the last one after.

  3. Exit Ticket (Stream Format):

    At the end of class, have students anonymously post "One thing you learned" and "One question you still have."

  4. Show and Tell (Wall or Shelf Format):

    For homework, students find a picture of an object that is important to them, upload it to the Padlet, and write two sentences about it. In class, you use the Padlet to lead a speaking activity.

  5. Jigsaw Reading (Shelf Format):

    Create columns for "Group 1," "Group 2," etc. Each group reads a different text and posts a summary of their text in their column for the other groups to read.

  6. Digital Portfolio (Shelf Format):

    Create a private Padlet for a single student. Create columns for "Writing," "Videos," "Audio." The student uploads their work throughout the semester. Use the "Comment" feature to give feedback.

  7. Collaborative Story (Timeline Format):

    Write the first sentence of a story. The next student adds a post with the next sentence, and so on, building a story along the timeline.

  8. Class Q&A Board (Wall Format):

    Keep a Padlet running for the whole semester where students can post anonymous questions about homework, grammar, or exams.

  9. Listening/Video Task (Shelf Format):

    In Column 1, post a YouTube video. In Column 2 ("Pre-Listening"), ask students to predict the content. In Column 3 ("Post-Listening"), add comprehension or discussion questions for them to answer.

  10. Travel Guide (Map Format):

    For a unit on travel, have each student choose a city. They must add a pin to the map and, in their post, write a short recommendation (what to see, what to eat) for that city.
