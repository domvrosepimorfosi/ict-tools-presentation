Comprehensive Guide to Creating a Genially

Genially (pronounced "jee-nee-ally") is a free online tool for creating interactive content. Think of it as "PowerPoint on steroids." It allows you to create presentations, infographics, interactive images, and quizzes that are far more engaging than static documents.

Instead of just linear slides, you can add layers of information, pop-up windows, links, and quizzes directly *inside* your creation.

Key Advantages for EFL

  • All-in-One: You can embed videos, audio, quizzes (like Quizizz/Forms), and Google Maps all into one single presentation.
  • Interactivity: This is its biggest strength. You can make an image of a room and have students click on the "sofa" to see a pop-up with the word, or click on a "lamp" to hear an audio file.
  • Gamification: Excellent for creating "Escape Room" games or "Choose Your Own Adventure" stories, which are incredibly motivating for students.
  • Visual Appeal: It's template-based, so your creations look professional and dynamic with very little effort.
  • Student-Paced: Students can explore the content at their own speed, clicking on the elements that interest them.

The free plan is very generous and allows you to create unlimited projects. The main limitations are that you can't download your projects for offline use (they must be shared via a link) and you can't access premium templates.

  1. Sign Up: Go to genial.ly and sign up for a free "Education" account. You can use your Google, Microsoft, or any email account.
  2. Create Your Genially: Once on your dashboard, click the "Create Genially" button (usually blue, top left).
  3. Choose a Template (The Fun Part): You will now see many categories.
    • Presentation: Best for interactive lessons, like a PowerPoint.
    • Gamification: The best one for EFL! This has templates for quizzes, breakout games, and escape rooms.
    • Interactive Image: Perfect for vocabulary. You upload one image (like a map, a street scene, or a diagram) and add interactive "hotspots" (buttons) to it.
    • Infographic: Good for presenting grammar rules or project steps visually.
    • Blank Creation: For when you're feeling adventurous.
  4. Filter by "Free": Make sure to click the "Free" filter at the top to only see the templates you can use on the basic plan.
  5. Select a template you like and click "Use this template".

The editor looks a bit like PowerPoint. On the left, you have your main tools. On the right, you see your slides (pages). The "magic" of Genially comes from two simple buttons:

1. The "Interactivity" Button (The Pointing Finger üëÜ)

This is the most important button. When you click on *any* object (an image, a piece of text, a shape), you will see a small menu appear above it. Click the pointing finger icon. This opens the "Interactivity" menu, where you can choose what happens when a student clicks that object:

  • Tooltip: A small black box appears when you *hover* over the item. (Good for quick definitions).
  • Window (Most Useful): A large pop-up window appears when you *click* the item. You can put text, images, and videos inside this window.
  • Go to page: Turns the object into a button that jumps to another slide in your presentation. (This is the key to making "Choose Your Own Adventure" stories).
  • Link: Opens a new website tab.

2. The "Animation" Button (The Comet üí´)

Click on any object and then click the comet icon. This lets you add animations, just like in PowerPoint (e.g., "Fade In," "Bounce," "Pulse").

Pro Tip: Use the "Pulse" animation on your clickable items to show students that they are supposed to click on them!

The Left-Side Menu

  • Text: Add titles and text boxes.
  • Image: Upload your own images or search the built-in library of (free) Unsplash and Pixabay photos.
  • Resources: Add icons, shapes, and arrows.
  • Interactive Elements (Very Important): This is a library of pre-made buttons (like "+", "i", "home") that are designed to be clickable. Use these to make your project look clean and professional.
  • Smartblocks: Pre-made layouts for charts and diagrams.
  • Insert: Embed audio (you can record your own voice!), video (from YouTube/Vimeo), or other content (like a Google Form or Quizizz).
  • Pages: View all your slides and add new ones.
  1. When you are finished, click the "All Set" or "Present" button (top right).
  2. It will ask you to publish. Make sure it is set to "Public on the web" (this is the only option for free plans).
  3. Give it a title and description.
  4. Click "All Set".
  5. A new window will appear. Click the "Share" tab.
  6. This gives you the link (e.g., `view.genial.ly/12345...`).
  7. This is the link you copy and send to your students. They just open it in any browser to view and interact with your creation.

1. Interactive Vocabulary Scene (Interactive Image)

Upload a single, busy image (e.g., a "kitchen," "airport," or "street scene"). Add interactive buttons (+) over key objects. When students click a button, a "Window" pops up showing the word, a definition, and maybe an audio file of you pronouncing it.

2. Digital Escape Room (Gamification Template)

Use a "Breakout" or "Escape Room" template. These are pre-built games where students are "locked" in a room. To "escape," they must solve a series of puzzles. You just edit the puzzles to be grammar or vocabulary questions. This is incredibly high-engagement.

3. Choose Your Own Adventure (Presentation Template)

Create a story. On page 1, the student faces a choice (e.g., "You see a dark cave. Do you... A) Go inside, or B) Walk away?"). Make "A" and "B" clickable buttons. The "Go to page" interactivity will send them to different slides based on their choice, leading to different story outcomes.

4. Interactive Lesson (Presentation Template)

Instead of a boring PowerPoint, put your grammar rules inside clickable "Window" elements. Students can explore the lesson in the order they want. You can embed a YouTube video explaining the topic, followed by an embedded Google Form quiz, all on *one* slide.

5. Project Hub (Blank Creation)

Use the "Canvas" format to create a mind map for a class project. You can have the central topic, and then add boxes for "Links," "Videos," "Tasks," and "Examples." Students can add their own findings to the canvas.
